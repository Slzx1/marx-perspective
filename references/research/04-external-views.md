## 1. Economic Critiques of _Capital_

### 1.1 Eugen von Bohm-Bawerk (1851–1914)

**Work**: _Karl Marx and the Close of His System_ (1896), _Capital and Interest_ (1884)
**Status**: [Well-established] — the most influential early economic critic of Marx's value theory.

**Core criticisms**:

1. **Failure to prove the labor theory of value**: Bohm-Bawerk argued Marx only proposed two legitimate paths to a theory of value — psychological analysis of exchange motives, or empirical study of exchange relations — and pursued neither. Instead, Marx relied on "dialectical tricks" and "a simple play on words." [Active debate]

2. **The abstraction problem**: Marx argued that when commodities are stripped of use-values, only abstract labor remains as common property. Bohm-Bawerk countered that commodities share other common properties: scarcity, being objects of demand, being products of nature, and possessing abstract utility (capacity to satisfy human wants). Marx, he charged, arbitrarily selected labor while ignoring these. [Well-established]

3. **The contradiction between Volume I and Volume III**: Marx's Volume I says commodities exchange at labor values; Volume III says they exchange at prices of production that deviate from values. Bohm-Bawerk declared this the self-contradiction that collapsed Marx's system — "the belated bad harvest that was supposed to grow out of bad sowing." [Active debate]

**Academic rebuttals**:

| Defender | Work | Key Argument |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **Rudolf Hilferding** (1904) | _Bohm-Bawerk's Marx-Critique_ | Bohm-Bawerk fundamentally misunderstood Marx's theory as **psychological and ahistorical**; Marx's value theory is **sociological and historical** —value is a social relation specific to commodity-producing societies, not a subjective utility judgment |
| **I.I. Rubin** (1928) | _Essays on Marx's Theory of Value_ | Bohm-Bawerk read only Marx's first five pages and missed the essential grounding: the **theory of commodity fetishism**, which reveals that production relations among people necessarily take the material form of value relations |
| **Louis B. Boudin** (1907) | _The Theoretical System of Karl Marx_ | A constant factor (human psychology) cannot explain historically specific capitalist phenomena; Marx deliberately avoided psychological motives for this reason |
| **Fabio Petri** (2024) | Centro Sraffa Working Paper 65 | While the claim that labor is the "substance" of value may need to be abandoned under Sraffian price theory, Marx's analyses of **fetishism** and **concrete/abstract labor** remain valid |

**Source reliability**: High. Bohm-Bawerk's text is a primary source of Austrian economics' critique of Marx; Hilferding's response is a classic Marxist defense.

### 1.2 Paul Samuelson (1915–2009)

**Work**: "Understanding the Marxian Notion of Exploitation" (_Journal of Economic Literature_, 1971)
**Status**: [Well-established] — the most prominent neoclassical critique.

**Core criticisms**:

1. **"Erase and replace"**: Samuelson characterized Marx's transformation of values into prices as writing down one system (values), erasing it, and filling in another (prices) — an empty logical exercise, not a genuine transformation. [Active debate]

2. **Redundancy of values**: Competitive prices and the rate of profit can be determined **directly from physical production coefficients and the real wage bundle**, without any reference to labor values. Volume I's value analysis is "an unnecessary and obfuscating detour." [Active debate]

3. **Mutually exclusive alternatives**: Values and prices are not two interconnected systems where one derives from the other; they are **competing explanations** of the same phenomena, and the price system is superior.

**Academic rebuttals**:

| Defender | Work | Key Argument |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **Paul Mattick** (1972) | _Science & Society_ | Samuelson misunderstood Marx's **purpose**: it was never to derive empirical market prices from values, but to show how the **social product divides into value and surplus value** at the aggregate level, regardless of how competitive prices deviate |
| **William Baumol** (1974) | _Journal of Economic Literature_ | Marx's value theory was **never intended to determine relative prices** (a Ricardian project); its purpose was to explain the **origin of profits** as unpaid surplus labor. The transformation is peripheral to Marx's main goal |
| **Ronald Meek** (1977) | "From Values to Prices" | Acknowledges Samuelson's technical point about redundancy **if price determination is the only goal**, but argues Marx's journey through value categories was necessary to reveal **social relations of production** that prices conceal |
| **Michio Morishima** (1974) | _Marx's Economics_ | The **Fundamental Marxian Theorem** — positive profits exist if and only if the rate of surplus value is positive — provides a formal, mathematical defense of the Marxian exploitation concept, even in a general equilibrium framework |

**Meta-assessment** [Scholarly consensus]: There is broad agreement that Samuelson's **mathematical point is technically valid within a static equilibrium framework** — prices of production can indeed be computed without value magnitudes. The unresolved dispute is whether Marx's value categories are **analytically essential** for understanding capitalism's laws of motion, exploitation, and crisis tendencies, or a superfluous metaphysical encumbrance.

**Source reliability**: High. Samuelson's paper in _JEL_ is peer-reviewed; the responses from Mattick, Baumol, Meek, and Morishima are all from major academic venues.

### 1.3 Joseph Schumpeter (1883–1950)

**Work**: _Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy_ (1942) — Part I: "The Marxian Doctrine"
**Status**: [Well-established] — the most balanced and sympathetic major critique from a mainstream economist.

**Schumpeter's four frames**:

| Frame | Assessment |
|-------|-----------|
| **Marx the Prophet** | Acknowledged Marx's powerful appeal — he offered a vision of inevitable historical redemption that resonated with deep human needs |
| **Marx the Sociologist** | **Praised** Marx's theory of class as a genuine intellectual achievement; noted its kinship with Weberian class analysis; credited Marx with achieving a synthesis surpassing Comte and Spencer |
| **Marx the Economist** | **Critical**: Marx's economic theory is excessively "stationary"; crucially, Marx failed to distinguish the **capitalist** (who owns capital) from the **entrepreneur** (who innovates) — a distinction central to Schumpeter's own theory of creative destruction |
| **Marx the Teacher** | Mixed evaluation: Marx's theories better fit English/Dutch colonial experiences than, say, New England; social structures "do not readily melt" as the economic interpretation of history would predict |

**Key insight**: Schumpeter rendered Marx's economics a secondary concern while simultaneously elevating Marx the sociologist — an evaluation that mainstream economics largely ignored and heterodox Marxists largely accepted. [Contested]

**Further note**: Schumpeter predicted capitalism would collapse not from failure (as Marx argued) but from **success** — its very efficiency would erode the social and institutional supports (entrepreneurial drive, bourgeois family, protective political strata) that sustain it. This is an **inversion**, not a refutation, of Marx's prediction. A Cambridge University Press article (_European Journal of Political Research_) also notes that Schumpeter **mischaracterized Marx's conception of socialism**: Marx's socialism was not state ownership/nationalization but the abolition of commodities and the wage system, making Marx more democratic than Schumpeter suggested.

**Source reliability**: Very high. _Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy_ is the third most cited book in the social sciences published before 1950, behind Marx's _Capital_ and Smith's _Wealth of Nations_.

## 2. Philosophical Evaluations of Marx

### 2.1 Hannah Arendt (1906–1975)

**Work**: _The Human Condition_ (1958)
**Status**: [Well-established] — a major philosophical critique from a non-Marxist (though Arendt's earlier work with Heidegger and her Jewish identity complicate simple categorization).

**Core critique — the labor/work/action distinction**:

| Activity | Character | Realm | Freedom? |
|----------|-----------|-------|----------|
| **Labor** | Repetitive, cyclical, tied to biological necessity | Private/necessity | No |
| **Work** | Creates durable objects (_homo faber_); instrumental | Man-made world | Partial |
| **Action** | Speech and deeds among plural individuals | Public/political | **Yes** |

Arendt's charge: Marx **conflated labor and work**, elevating _animal laborans_ to the center of political thought. By glorifying labor as the essence of human species-being, Marx:

1. **Subordinated freedom to necessity** — Labor is governed by biological compulsion, not free initiative.
2. **Eroded the public realm** — When labor enters the public square, politics becomes administration of economic needs, not genuine deliberation.
3. **Enabled totalitarianism** — She saw a direct line from Marx's reduction of humans to _animal laborans_ to totalitarian homogenization.

> "In all stages of his work, [Marx] defines man as _animal laborans_ to then lead him into a society in which this power... is no longer necessary. We are left with the distressing alternative between productive slavery and unproductive freedom." — Arendt, _The Human Condition_

**Academic rebuttals**:

- **Marx distinguished alienated labor from free labor**: The labor Marx criticizes in capitalism (forced, external, degrading) is precisely what Arendt describes. Marx's emancipatory vision points toward labor as **self-realization**, not mere subsistence. [Active debate]
- **Arendt ignores Marx's "species-being"**: In the 1844 Manuscripts, Marx describes labor as "life-activity, productive life itself," through which humans realize their capabilities — closer to Arendt's "action" than she acknowledges.
- **Feminist critique of Arendt** (Seyla Benhabib, et al.): Arendt's tripartite division has an **aristocratic bias** — the Greek _polis_ where free male citizens acted politically existed precisely because slaves and women performed labor. This mirrors the very exclusion of social reproduction for which Marx is criticized.
- **Work is inherently social**: Wage labor involves cooperation, mutual recognition, and normative commitments — a genuinely intersubjective domain that Arendt misses by casting work as solitary fabrication.

**Source reliability**: Very high. _The Human Condition_ is a canonical text of 20th-century political philosophy.

### 2.2 Karl Popper (1902–1994)

**Works**: _The Poverty of Historicism_ (1957), _The Open Society and Its Enemies_ (1945)
**Status**: [Well-established] — the most influential epistemological critique of Marx.

**Core arguments**:

1. **The impossibility of historical prediction**: The course of history is influenced by the growth of knowledge; we cannot predict the future growth of knowledge (doing so would require knowing today what we will only know tomorrow); therefore, we cannot predict the future course of history; therefore, no theoretical history (including historical materialism) is possible. [Active debate]

2. **Confusion of trends with laws**: Popper acknowledged historical trends exist but insisted they are not universal laws. Trends that persist for centuries can reverse within a decade; they depend on specific initial conditions that cannot be guaranteed.

3. **Falsifiability and pseudo-science**: Marxist prophecies (the inevitable advent of socialism) are not genuinely scientific because they are not falsifiable — they can perpetually claim fulfillment "in the future" without ever facing a decisive empirical test.

4. **The "open society" vs. "closed society"**: Marx's historicist determinism leads logically toward **totalitarianism** because it subordinates individual moral responsibility and critical freedom to the supposed "inevitable" direction of history.

**Nuance often omitted**:

- Popper **distinguished Marx from other historicists** (Plato, Hegel). He expressed genuine **sympathy for Marx's moral motivations** — revulsion at working-class suffering — which he did not extend to Plato or Hegel.
- He considered Marx's economic interpretation of history a **"valuable advance"** in social science.
- His real target was **historicism as an intellectual attitude**, not Marx the humanitarian.

**Academic rebuttals**:

| Critic | Core Rebuttal |
|--------|---------------|
| **Maurice Cornforth** (1968) | Popper misunderstood Marx's method and dialectical materialism; his "open society" is itself ideological |
| **Omar Herrera Rodriguez** (2025) | Popper's criticism of Marx's method is "false" and "inconsistent" |
| **Wei Xu** (2023, CASS) | Popper's "Achilles' heel": he fails to prove history is **entirely** determined by knowledge growth; Marx's structural laws (productive forces -> relations of production) operate independently of knowledge growth |
| **Martyn Hammersley** (2024-25, Open University) | Popper shared more with his targets — socialist leanings, commitment to science-based policy — than is often recognized |

**Source reliability**: Very high. Popper's works are among the most cited in 20th-century philosophy of science.

### 2.3 Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980)

**Work**: _Critique of Dialectical Reason_ (1960), _Search for a Method_ (1957)
**Status**: [Well-established] — the major attempt to fuse existentialism with Marxism.

**Sartre's position**:

- Declared Marxism "the philosophy of our time" and existentialism a subordinate "ideology" within it.
- Marxism had become sclerotic — "lazy Marxism" that dissolved individuals into abstract economic categories. Existentialism's task was to **recall Marxism to its own suppressed foundations** in lived experience.
- Key concepts: **praxis** (goal-oriented human action), **the practico-inert** (ossified human activity confronting individuals as alien force), **seriality** (atomized collective existence), **the group-in-fusion** (genuinely collective revolutionary subject).
- Sartre presented Marx as synthesizing **Hegel (objectification/sociality) and Kierkegaard (finite subjectivity)**.

**Academic evaluation**:

- **Joseph Catalano**: The _Critique_ represents **development rather than break** with Sartre's earlier existentialism — it adds the historical dimension absent from _Being and Nothingness_ while retaining the core commitment to freedom.
- **Pietro Chiodi** (1965): Sartre's fundamental departure from Marx lies in **identifying alienation with objectification** — a corollary of any philosophy that "accords a privileged status to the subjective." Sartre's existentialist roots fatally compromise his Marxism. [Contested]
- **Dick Howard** (1973): Sartre's _Critique_ functions as a **Kantian prolegomena** to future anthropology — it provides conditions of possibility for dialectical reason, not empirical history. However, Sartre's insistence on constitutive primacy of free individual praxis makes consciousness "too thin."
- **James Miller** (1979): Sartre restored the Kierkegaardian pole of Marx — the irreducible finitude and subjectivity of human beings — which latter-day Marxism had abandoned for "purely natural dialectic."

**Source reliability**: High. Sartre is a major 20th-century philosopher; the critical literature on his Marxism is extensive.

### 2.4 Louis Althusser (1918–1990)

**Works**: _For Marx_ (1965), _Reading Capital_ (1965, with Balibar et al.)
**Status**: [Well-established] — the most influential structuralist reinterpretation of Marx.

**Core theses**:

1. **The "epistemological break" (_coupure epistemologique_)**: Borrowed from Gaston Bachelard. Marx's thought underwent a radical rupture around **1845** (_Theses on Feuerbach_, _The German Ideology_), dividing his work into:
   - **Pre-1845**: Ideology — humanism, Hegelianism, Feuerbachian "species-being"
   - **Post-1845**: Science — historical materialism, the science of social formations

2. **The "problematic" (_problematique_)**: The underlying conceptual structure that determines what questions can be asked. Young Marx's problematic = "Man"/human essence; Mature Marx's problematic = the mode of production and the "ensemble of social relations."

3. **Theoretical anti-humanism**: The mature Marx abandoned "human essence" entirely. "Man" cannot serve as theoretical foundation for scientific analysis of society. This is **not** indifference to human suffering but recognition that "human nature" is not a scientific concept.

4. **Overdetermination and structural causality**: Social formations are complex structured wholes where economic determination operates "in the last instance" through the relative autonomy of political, ideological, and theoretical levels.

**Criticisms of Althusser**:

- **Overly rigid dichotomy**: Themes of alienation persist in _Capital_ (e.g., commodity fetishism uses the same Feuerbachian analogy of religion that the 1844 Manuscripts used). [Active debate]
- **Theoretical extremism**: By opposing science and ideology absolutely, Althusser risks making Marxism a bloodless structuralism that erases human agency.
- **Self-criticism**: Althusser himself later moderated — in _Elements of Self-Criticism_ (1974) he acknowledged the sharp science/ideology opposition was a "theoreticist" error.
- **Historical evidence**: Marx attempted to publish his early works in 1851 and referenced his early Hegel critique in the 1871 _Capital_ afterword, suggesting he did not repudiate his earlier work entirely.

**Source reliability**: Very high. Althusser's interpretation, though controversial, remains one of the most influential frameworks in Marxist philosophy.

### 2.5 G.A. Cohen (1941–2009) and Analytical Marxism

**Work**: _Karl Marx's Theory of History: A Defence_ (1978)
**Status**: [Well-established] — founding text of Analytical Marxism; defended an "old-fashioned" technological-determinist historical materialism using analytic philosophy.

**Cohen's thesis — "productive force determinism"**:
- Material forces of production (technology, tools, human skills) **functionally explain** economic relations of production.
- Economic relations of production **functionally explain** the legal-political superstructure.
- Transitions between modes of production are driven by transhistorical human rationality — people tend to adopt more productive technologies to reduce the burden of labor.

**The September Group**: Cohen, Jon Elster, John Roemer, Adam Przeworski, Erik Olin Wright, Philippe Van Parijs. Shared method: analytic philosophy, neoclassical economics, rational choice theory, game theory, methodological individualism.

**The unraveling — criticism from within and without**:

| Critic | Argument |
|--------|----------|
| **Jon Elster** (1985) | Marx's economics are "multiply confused"; most Marxist claims fail rational-choice reconstruction |
| **Andrew Levine & Erik Olin Wright** | Cohen overlooked the primacy of **class actors** — relations of production (class struggle) have primacy over productive forces |
| **Richard W. Miller** | Counterposed a "mode of production" interpretation emphasizing class struggle, not technology |
| **Sean Sayers** | Analytical Marxism **eviscerated** Marxism by stripping it of the dialectical method Marx insisted was fundamental |
| **Marcus Roberts** (1997) | After two decades, "few Marxist 'insights' survived the attempt to 'reconstruct' it"; the project marked **the end of attempts to formulate a defensible traditional historical materialism** |
| **Fabien Tarrit** (2020) | The project **failed** — it neither attracted scholars beyond its zone nor successfully defended Marxist positions |

**Cohen's later trajectory**: From defending orthodox historical materialism (1978) -> doubts and modifications (1988) -> normative turn confronting Nozick (_Self-Ownership, Freedom and Equality_, 1995) -> egalitarian moral philosophy (_If You're an Egalitarian How Come You're So Rich?_, 2000). Widely read as a gradual abandonment of Marxism's explanatory ambitions in favor of left-liberal normative political philosophy.

**The self-ownership paradox**:
- Cohen argued Marxist critique of exploitation rests on the **self-ownership principle** — the **same principle** at the heart of Nozick's libertarianism.
- This creates a devastating inconsistency: taxing workers for welfare programs violates self-ownership for the same reasons capitalist exploitation does.
- Cohen's attempt to defeat Nozick on libertarian terms was a "Pyrrhic victory."

**Source reliability**: Very high. Cohen, Elster, and Roemer published in top academic presses and journals.

### 2.6 Leszek Kolakowski (1927–2009)

**Work**: _Main Currents of Marxism_ (3 vols., 1976/1978)
**Status**: [Well-established] — widely regarded as the most comprehensive single-author history of Marxist thought.

**Core arguments**:

1. **Three motifs of Marxism**: Marx's thought is held together by three intellectual traditions — **Romantic** (critique of dehumanization, nostalgia for organic community), **Promethean-Faustian** (faith in humanity's self-creating power, collective redemption through the proletariat), and **Enlightenment Rationalist/Determinist** (belief in objective historical laws).

2. **Marxism as secularized religion**: Marx's philosophy is a secularization of **Christian eschatology** — human essence alienated from existence, history moving toward final reconciliation (communism), the proletariat as collective redeemer.

3. **Continuity thesis (Lukacsian)**: There is **no fundamental break** between young and mature Marx; _Capital_ is a working-out of the 1844 Manuscripts' analysis of alienation.

4. **Leninism -> Stalinism as logical continuation**: Stalinism is not a "betrayal" or "distortion" of Leninism but **one possible — even coherent — interpretation** of Leninist principles, especially regarding the vanguard party and dictatorship of the proletariat.

5. **Final verdict**: Marxism is "the greatest fantasy of the twentieth century."

**Academic criticisms**:

- **Hostile partisanship**: Kolakowski's personal disillusionment with Stalinism fatally compromises objectivity. The tone becomes "vituperative and ungenerous" (Martin Jay) toward Western Marxists like the Frankfurt School.
- **Lukacsian interpretive straightjacket**: Kolakowski presents his analysis as neutral while taking sides in intra-Marxist debates and using his own interpretive choice to condemn Marxism as a whole.
- **Neglect of Marxist economics and social science** (Mark Blaug, Craig Calhoun): Treats Marxism "primarily in philosophical and political terms, thereby distorting the pivotal role of economic theory."
- **The "logical continuation" thesis disputed**: Alternative Marxist traditions (Luxemburg, council communism, Austro-Marxism, Gramsci) resist reduction to Soviet tyranny.

**Positive reception**: A.J. Ayer declined to discuss 20th-century Marxist philosophy because he "could not improve upon Kolakowski's treatment." Sidney Hook called it the most comprehensive treatment of Marx. Richard Rorty said Kolakowski tells you "pretty much all you will ever need to know about Marx and Marxism-Leninism."

**Source reliability**: Very high, though the work's strong partisan perspective requires reading alongside other interpretations.

## 3. Biographical Portraits of Marx

### 3.1 Franz Mehring — _Karl Marx: The Story of His Life_ (1918)

**Status**: [Well-established] — the first major biography; the "classical" Marxist biography.

- **Perspective**: Mehring was a German Social Democrat, historian, and Marx-Engels biographer. His biography is the **first comprehensive life**, written while many who knew Marx personally were still alive.
- **Strengths**: Detailed on Marx's political activities, journalistic work, and the 1848 revolution. Rich on context of the German socialist movement.
- **Limitations**: Mehring was not granted full access to Marx's unpublished manuscripts; he could not treat the early philosophical works (the 1844 Manuscripts were not yet published). His portrait is that of a **party-founder** — a usable political ancestor for the SPD. [Contested - seen as hagiographic by some critics]

**Source reliability**: High as a primary historical source on Marx's political biography, but limited by lack of access to crucial manuscripts.

---

### 3.2 Isaiah Berlin — _Karl Marx: His Life and Environment_ (1939)

**Status**: [Well-established] — the most elegant and enduring concise intellectual biography.

- **Perspective**: Liberal pluralist. Berlin wrote it at age 30, admitting he "had never read a line of Marx" before accepting the commission.
- **Strengths**: "Admirable ability to translate many abstruse and obscure notions of Marxism into clear language" (Kolakowski). "The best brief account of the life and thought of Marx" (_Saturday Review_). A "model of objective clarity" (TLS).
- **Limitations** (per David Leopold, Oxford):
  - **Over-systematization**: Berlin saw Marx's work as having a "massive architectonic quality" when it was changing, contradictory, and unfinished.
  - **Premature system**: Identifies Marx's "final" intellectual transformation as occurring before Marx left Paris in 1845, when he was in his twenties.
  - **Isolation from British context**: Berlin quipped Marx "might just as well have spent his exile on Madagascar" — a "brilliant quip but poor intellectual biography"; British political and literary culture significantly influenced Marx.
  - **Denial of moral critique**: Berlin insists Marx makes "no moral complaints about capitalism," ignoring the extensive language of "theft" and "robbery" in _Capital_.
  - **Failure of empathy (_Einfuhlung_)**: Despite his reputation as a master of empathetic understanding, Berlin failed to achieve it with Marx.

**Source reliability**: High as a work of intellectual history/biography, but Berlin's Marx is as much a reflection of his own liberal pluralist preoccupations as it is a transparent portrait.

---

### 3.3 David McLellan — _Karl Marx: His Life and Thought_ (1973)

**Status**: [Scholarly consensus] — widely considered the standard scholarly biography.

- **Perspective**: Sympathetic but critical; thorough; based on extensive archival research. McLellan is a leading English-language Marx scholar.
- **Strengths**: Comprehensive integration of life and intellectual development. Strong on Marx's intellectual debts to Hegel, Feuerbach, and the Young Hegelians. Balanced treatment of the political and the theoretical. Covers Marx's journalism, his family life, his relationship with Engels without hagiography.
- **Limitations**: Published before the full MEGA (Marx-Engels-Gesamtausgabe) editorial project made available all of Marx's notebooks and marginalia. Some details have been superseded by subsequent archival discoveries.
- **Continuity thesis**: McLellan is generally associated with the view that Marx's thought shows **continuity** between the early humanist writings and the mature economic critique.

**Source reliability**: Very high. McLellan's biography remains a standard reference in Marx scholarship.

---

### 3.4 Jonathan Sperber — _Karl Marx: A Nineteenth-Century Life_ (2013)

**Status**: [Well-established] — the major recent biography, distinguished by archival depth and historical contextualization.

- **Perspective**: A historian of 19th-century Europe (University of Missouri), Sperber insists Marx must be understood as **exclusively a 19th-century figure**, not a prophet of 20th-century communism. He seeks to "bury" Marx in his own century.
- **Strengths**: Masterful use of MEGA archival sources. Rich, accessible narrative. Successfully **demythologizes** Marx — humanizes him as a passionate, perpetually indebted, combative radical. Vivid on Marx's private life: his love for Jenny, his family tragedies, the harrowing poverty of London exile.
- **Academic reviews**:
  - **Tristram Hunt** (_Guardian_): "A brilliant embedding of Marx in his times... profoundly important."
  - **Diana Siclovan** (Institute of Historical Research): Praises accessibility and detail; minor criticism that the third section is less coherent and Sperber over-stresses Marx's financial mismanagement.
  - **Karl Christian Fuhrer** (_H-Soz-u-Kult_): Credits Sperber with demythologizing Marx but notes a significant gap — Sperber largely **ignores the Industrial Revolution's social consequences**, failing to convey the miserable working conditions that gave Marx's ideas their moral force.
  - **Howard A. Doughty** (_College Quarterly_): Argues Sperber's aim is to "bury" Marx in the 19th century and deny his contemporary relevance. "No credible analysis of modern civilization can proceed without engaging Marx's thought, regardless of failed predictions."
  - **Peter Luff** (Reitaku University): Sperber's insight into Marx's **personality** falls short — particularly on the sources of Marx's atheism and the significance of his relationships.

**Source reliability**: Very high. Uses the MEGA archives. Published by Liveright (Norton). The central thesis — that Marx belongs entirely to the 19th century — is [Contested].

## 4. Methodological Disputes: Labor Theory of Value and the Transformation Problem

### 4.1 The Transformation Problem — Historical Phases

**Phase 1 (1894–1906): Critique of Contradiction**
- **Bohm-Bawerk** (1896): The contradiction between Volume I (exchange at values) and Volume III (exchange at prices of production) is fatal to Marx's system.
- **Bortkiewicz** (1906–1907): First formal mathematical treatment. Showed Marx's transformation could be corrected — but only by sacrificing **one** of Marx's two aggregate equalities (total value = total prices OR total surplus value = total profit, but not both simultaneously).

**Phase 2 (1906–1971): The Search for a Solution**
- **Seton** (1957), **Morishima-Seton** (1961): Both Marxian equalities can hold only under very restrictive conditions (equal organic composition of capital across sectors), which is empirically unrealistic.

**Phase 3 (1971–present): The Critique of Redundancy**
- **Sraffa** (1960): _Production of Commodities by Means of Commodities_ — production prices can be derived from physical input-output data and income distribution with **no reference to labor values**.
- **Steedman** (1977, _Marx After Sraffa_): Sraffa's work "refutes the labor theory of value" while ironically supporting other Marxian conclusions (class antagonism, exploitation). Labor values are a redundant detour.
- **Samuelson** (1971): Values -> prices is an "erase and replace" exercise.

### 4.2 Major Defenses

**A. The "New Interpretation" / "New Solution" (1980s)**
**Foley, Dumenil, Lipietz**

- Redefines the **value of labor power**: not by labor embodied in the workers' consumption bundle, but by the **money wage relative to the Monetary Expression of Labor Time (MELT)**.
- Shifts focus from transforming individual commodity values to a **macro-monetary framework** where aggregate net product in value terms always equals aggregate value added.
- Preserves the core Marxian conclusion: **living labor is the sole source of new value**.

**B. The Temporal Single System Interpretation (TSSI) (1990s–present)**
**Kliman, McGlone, Freeman**

- Rejects the **dual-system** approach (separate value and price systems) and the static equilibrium framework imposed by critics.
- Argues Marx intended a **single-system** where values and prices are determined **temporally** — input prices are historical, not simultaneously determined with output prices.
- Claims that under Marx's actual method, **both aggregate equalities hold** and there is no logical inconsistency.
- **Kliman** (_Reclaiming Marx's Capital_, 2007): The TSSI's greatest merit is not to deliver a new analytical solution but "to claim that Marx's original method of dealing with the issue must be studied and regarded as a competing solution."

[Active debate] — The TSSI has been vigorously contested; critics argue it either proves too much (any arbitrary system can be "consistent" under its assumptions) or relies on definitions that depart from Marx's own.

**C. Qualitative / Value-Form Approaches (the "New Reading of Marx")**
**Heinrich, Backhaus, Arthur, Moseley, Rubin (precursor)**

- Shift focus from **quantitative** transformation to the **qualitative form of value** — why does labor take the form of value in capitalism at all?
- Value is not a technical quantity but a **social relation** that can only exist through money as the general equivalent (monetary theory of value).
- **Michael Heinrich**: Marx's goal was not to prove labor is the "substance of value" in a quantitative sense, but to "reconstruct the specifically social character of labor from the social form of the labor product."
- **Fred Moseley** (_Money and Totality_, 2016): A macro-monetary interpretation where the transformation is not from individual values to prices but from the **aggregate value of total surplus-value** to its distribution across sectors as profit.

**D. Empirical and Probabilistic Approaches**
**Farjoun & Machover** (_Laws of Chaos_, 1983); **Anwar Shaikh** (_Capitalism_, 2016); **Paul Cockshott**

- A deterministic, exact transformation is impossible and unnecessary; real capitalist economies are stochastic.
- **Shaikh**: Labor values are excellent predictors of market prices — typically explaining **over 90%** of price variation across many countries and time periods, even if the theoretical transformation is contested. This is a widely replicated empirical finding. [Well-established]
- **Cockshott**: Similarly demonstrated strong empirical regularities between labor content and price structures.

### 4.3 The Current State of the Debate

**Laibman** (2025) summarizes the impasse:

> "It has been hard for participants in these discussions to 'cross swords,' and a certain stasis has evolved between two broad non-communicating camps: 'true believers,' for whom Marx's texts are still the ultimate source for the truth about value, exploitation, and crisis; and 'stubborn skeptics,' for whom critical political economy must follow Sraffa in modeling the elements and ratios of the experienced economy, and must also deny the existence of a labor-value dimension."

**The labor theory of value is a tautology?** This charge has been leveled from multiple directions. The counter-argument: Marx's value theory is not a definition but an **explanation of a real social process** — the specific way in which labor becomes socially validated in a commodity-producing economy. The circularity disappears when one recognizes that value is not an _a priori_ definition but an emergent property of a historically specific social form. [Active debate]

## 5. The "Two Marxes" Debate

### 5.1 Origins

The controversy erupted after the posthumous publication of Marx's **Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844** (first published 1932), which revealed a philosophically rich, Hegelian-influenced, humanist Marx previously unknown to the first generation of Marxists (Kautsky, Plekhanov, Lenin).

### 5.2 The Discontinuity Thesis ("Two Marxes")

**Proponents**: Soviet orthodox Marxists, Louis Althusser, Sidney Hook, Daniel Bell, Lewis Feuer.

**Position**:
- A fundamental break occurred around 1845 (_Theses on Feuerbach_, _The German Ideology_).
- **Young Marx** (pre-1845): Trapped in Hegelian/Feuerbachian idealism; "human essence," "species-being," "alienation" — bourgeois humanist philosophy, not science.
- **Mature Marx** (post-1845): Scientific socialism — historical materialism, labor theory of value, class struggle — a structural, objective science free of ethical or anthropological presuppositions.
- "Alienation" as a philosophical concept was largely abandoned in favor of commodity fetishism, division of labor, and exploitation.

**Althusser's version**: The most rigorous formulation. Borrowed Bachelard's _coupure epistemologique_ to mark the science/ideology divide. Warned that the humanist revival around the early Marx was **politically motivated** — aimed at reconciling Marx with bourgeois philosophy and attacking Soviet orthodoxy.

### 5.3 The Continuity Thesis ("One Marx")

**Proponents**: Erich Fromm, Gyorgy Lukacs, Herbert Marcuse, David McLellan, Leszek Kolakowski, Shlomo Avineri, Istvan Meszaros.

**Position**:
- The **core philosophy** of the young Marx was **never abandoned**; the later _Capital_ is incomprehensible without it.
- The concept of alienation persists throughout. _Capital_'s section on "The Fetishism of Commodities" is essentially a restatement of the 1844 Manuscripts' alienation theory: "the social character of men's labor appears to them as an objective character stamped upon the product of that labor."
- Marx himself referred back to his early Hegel critique in the **1873 Afterword to _Capital_** — "nearly thirty years ago" — indicating he considered his early work the birthplace of mature Marxism.
- In _The German Ideology_, described "alienation" phenomena without the word: "man's own deed becomes an alien power opposed to him, which enslaves him instead of being controlled by him."
- In the _Grundrisse_ (1857–58): Marx returned to themes from the 1844 Manuscripts — "self-realization" and "true freedom."
- In _Capital_ Volume III: "Beyond [the realm of necessity] begins that development of human energy which is an end in itself, **the true realm of freedom**."

**Fromm's polemic**: The "two Marxes" thesis was "politically motivated" by Soviet Communists whose system "is in every way a contradiction of Marx's humanism."

### 5.4 Intermediate Positions

**Ernest Mandel**: Distinguished three positions:
1. No difference at all — _Capital_ already present in the 1844 Manuscripts.
2. Early Marx is **richer** — offers a more "total" and "integral" analysis of alienated labor with ethical, anthropological, and philosophical dimensions.
3. Early Marx is an **obstacle** — a "pre-Marxist" notion that had to be overcome.

**Ruy Fausto**: "Continuity in discontinuity" — humanism persists in later works but shifts from being the _foundation_ of critique to its _horizon_: it ceases to be the grounding premise and becomes the emancipatory goal implied by the critique itself.

**Gyorgy Markus** (Budapest School): Distinguished **four forms** of Marx's critical theory corresponding to different developmental phases — different but not absolutely opposed.

**Etienne Balibar**: Marx's works cannot be neatly divided — philosophy, economics, and historical intervention are "inextricably linked throughout Marx's life."

**Althusser's self-correction**: Later conceded traces of humanism, historicism, and Hegelianism persist in _Capital_; the epistemological break is better understood as a **process** rather than a single event.

### 5.5 Political Stakes

| Position | Political Implication |
|----------|----------------------|
| Discontinuity (scientific Marx) | Legitimates "orthodox" Marxism and Soviet "scientific socialism"; downplays humanist concerns as bourgeois ideology |
| Continuity (humanist Marx) | Basis for criticizing Stalinist dehumanization from _within_ Marxism; connects Marxism to broader humanist and existentialist traditions |
| Early Marx is richer | Justifies revisionist, democratic, or ethical forms of socialism prioritizing individual freedom |
| Late Marx supersedes early | Defends scientific, structural, revolutionary character of Marxism against "ethical" dilution |

### 5.6 Current Scholarly Consensus

[Scholarly consensus] The binary of "young Marx vs. mature Marx" is now widely seen as **oversimplified**. While Marx's vocabulary, method, and focus undoubtedly evolved, the most productive contemporary approaches recognize **phases and shifts** within a deep **continuity of purpose**: understanding and overcoming the dehumanizing structures of capitalist society.

## 6. Feminist, Postcolonial, and Ecological Re-readings

### 6.1 Feminist Critiques and Re-readings

**Silvia Federici — _Caliban and the Witch_ (2004)**

**Status**: [Well-established] — the most influential feminist expansion/partial critique of Marx's _Capital_.

**Core argument**: Marx naturalized reproductive labor. He recognized labor-power is a commodity produced _outside_ the capital-labor relation but never theorized the work of producing it (childbearing, cooking, cleaning, emotional care) as **work**. He assumed the wage covers reproducing the worker through purchased commodities, eliding the unwaged labor that transforms those commodities into a living working human being.

**Federici's expansion of Marx's primitive accumulation**:

| Marx's Account | Federici's Expansion |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Expropriation of land (enclosures) | **Witch-hunts** (15th-17th c.) as systematic attack on women's social power, knowledge, and bodily autonomy |
| Creation of the "free" waged worker | **Confinement of women to unpaid domestic labor**, naturalized as "labor of love" |
| Concentration of wealth | **Disciplining of the female body** as "breeding machine" for the workforce |
| Colonization | **Epistemicide** — destruction of women's healing, midwifery, and communal knowledge systems |

**Key concepts**:
- The **capitalist separation of production from reproduction**: not a pre-existing, trans-historical division but a historically specific capitalist construction. Pre-capitalist societies did not organize work along this rigid binary.
- The **"patriarchy of the wage"**: The wage-system grants working-class men material control over women's bodies and unpaid labor, structuring gender oppression _through_ class relations.
- Marx codified rather than interrogated this separation.

**Academic critiques of Federici**:

- **Sue Ferguson** (2022): Federici flattens Marx's productive/unproductive labor distinction, risking theoretical incoherence. Social reproductive labor is _necessary_ to capitalism but does not produce surplus value in Marx's strict sense. Federici also **misattributes** a quote from Adam Smith to Marx (from _Theories of Surplus Value_), claiming Marx "knew better."
- **Angela Davis** (_Women, Race and Class_, 1981): Federici's claim that all women are oppressed through domestic unwaged work does not account for Black women's distinct experiences under slavery and its aftermath.
- **Lise Vogel**: Reproductive labor should _not_ be conceptualized in value-production terms at all; domestic labor's relation to capital is mediated differently than waged labor's.

**Social Reproduction Theory (SRT)**, systematized by **Tithi Bhattacharya** (ed., 2017): The broader tradition Federici belongs to. Core insight: "labor human work is at the centre of the creation or reproduction of society as a whole." What the wage hides is the vast terrain of unwaged, feminized work on which capitalist accumulation depends.

**Nancy Fraser** ("Behind Marx's Hidden Abode," 2014): Capitalism is an institutionalized social order dependent on "hidden abodes" of social reproduction, nature, and political power that it simultaneously depends upon and destabilizes.

**Source reliability**: High. Federici's work is published by Autonomedia/PM Press and widely cited in feminist and Marxist scholarship. Academic critiques from Ferguson, Davis, and Vogel are from major venues.

---

### 6.2 Postcolonial Critiques

**Dipesh Chakrabarty — _Provincializing Europe_ (2000)**

**Status**: [Well-established] — the foundational text of the postcolonial critique of Marx's alleged Eurocentrism.

**Core argument**:
- Academic historical discourse treats "Europe as the sovereign theoretical subject of all histories." All non-Western histories become variations on a master narrative: "the history of Europe."
- Marx's own work contains a tension. He recognized capital's global reach, but his **methodological and epistemological claims** have been consistently read in historicist, Eurocentric ways.
- Chakrabarty specifically critiques Marx's aphorism from the _Grundrisse_: "The anatomy of the human is a key to the anatomy of the ape" — implying non-European societies are to be understood through European categories.
- Chakrabarty distinguishes **History 1** (capital's process of becoming, retrospectively posited by capital itself) from **History 2** (life-worlds that resist capital's totalizing logic — the residual, non-capitalist elements that constantly interrupt and destabilize capital).

**Vivek Chibber's counter-critique — _Postcolonial Theory and the Specter of Capital_ (2013)**:

- Subaltern Studies and Chakrabarty fundamentally **misrepresent capitalism's universal drive** and its relationship to modernity in the East.
- The central historical antagonism is the contradiction between **capital and labor**, not a binary between "European" and "non-European" life-worlds.
- Jettisoning "universalizing categories" makes the structurality of global capitalism unintelligible.
- Postcolonial theory's emphasis on fundamental difference risks implying that non-Western subjects are constitutively distinct, lacking the same basic interest in defending their well-being and dignity — ironically reproducing an Orientalist trope.

**Synthesis attempts — Alf Gunvald Nilsen** (2017, _Critical Sociology_):

- Chibber's defense of universal categories relies on an ontology presenting modernity as first emerging in the West and diffusing outward — **erasing the constitutive role of the non-Western world** (colonialism, unfree labor, transatlantic slavery) in the genesis of capitalism.
- Proposes recovering resources within Marxism itself: **Trotsky's theory of uneven and combined development** (via Justin Rosenberg) and **Jarius Banaji's critique of "formal abstractionism"** — to build a relational ontology where global capitalism is always already an interlocking, transcontinental process.

**Source reliability**: High. Chakrabarty (Princeton University Press), Chibber (Verso), Nilsen (_Critical Sociology_, peer-reviewed). The debate remains [Active].

### 6.3 Ecological Critiques and Re-readings

**The Schism within Eco-Marxism: Metabolic Rift School vs. World-Ecology**

| | **Metabolic Rift School** | **World-Ecology (Moore)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Key figures** | John Bellamy Foster, Paul Burkett, Andreas Malm, Kohei Saito | Jason W. Moore |
| **Core concept** | Capitalism produces a "rift" in the universal metabolism of nature — rupture between social and natural systems | Capitalism is itself an ecological regime; no nature "outside" capitalism that it merely acts upon |
| **Ontological stance** | Analytical dualism (nature and society are distinct and can be in collision) | Relational monism (nature and society are always already entangled) |
| **Periodization** | Rift deepens decisively with fossil capital (~1830s steam) | Capitalocene begins in the long 16th century (~1450-1700) with Cheap Nature |
| **Value theory** | Labor alone creates value; nature is objective presupposition of production | Value operates through appropriation of "Cheap Nature" (food, labor, energy, raw materials) kept "off the books" |
| **On Marx's ecology** | Marx had fully-fledged ecological materialism (engagement with Liebig's soil chemistry); later obscured partly by Engels' editorial hand | Rift school's Marx still trapped in Cartesian binaries |

**Key figures**:

- **John Bellamy Foster** (_Marx's Ecology_, 2000): Recovered Marx's concept of _Stoffwechsel_ (metabolism) from his engagement with Liebig's soil chemistry, arguing Marx had a sophisticated ecological materialism. The "metabolic rift" is the central ecological concept Marx developed.

- **Andreas Malm** (_Fossil Capital_, 2016): Locates capital's turn to fossil fuels in the 1830s ascendancy of steam power in British cotton mills. Argues this was not about resource scarcity (water was abundant) but about **labor discipline** — steam allowed factories to be located in cities with surplus labor rather than being tied to rivers.

- **Kohei Saito** (_Karl Marx's Ecosocialism_, 2017; _Marx in the Anthropocene_, 2023): Through detailed MEGA archival study, Saito argues Marx's later notebooks show an increasingly ecological turn — including studies of soil depletion, deforestation, and a growing skepticism toward the idea of limitless productive force development. Saito controversially argues Engels' editorial interventions in Volumes II-III of _Capital_ obscured Marx's ecological insights. [Active debate] [Contested — the "Engels obscured Marx" claim is highly contentious]

- **Jason W. Moore** (_Capitalism in the Web of Life_, 2015): Rejects the rift framework as insufficient. Capitalism did not create a rift in nature; capitalism **is** a way of organizing nature. His key concept of "Cheap Nature" (the Four Cheaps: food, labor-power, energy, raw materials) proposes that capital can only accumulate by keeping these inputs "off the books" of formal value exchange.

**The debate between the camps**:

Foster/Burkett ("Value Isn't Everything," _Monthly Review_, 2018) directly attack Moore for treating value as a "hybrid actant" rather than a purely social form. They argue that glossing over the exclusive role of abstract labor in value-creation loses analytical grip on the specific capitalist drive that widens the metabolic rift.

Moore's camp suggests the rift school's dualism leads to a "fossil fixation" that cannot adequately grasp the systemic character of the crisis.

**Source reliability**: High. Foster (_Monthly Review_ Press), Malm (Verso), Saito (Cambridge University Press, MEGA-based), Moore (Verso). The intra-eco-Marxist debate is [Active].

**Critique from Degrowth**:

Some ecological thinkers argue Marx was fundamentally **productivist** — his vision of communism as unleashing productive forces from capitalist fetters is incompatible with ecological limits. Saito is the most prominent scholar arguing that Marx's **later notebooks** show he was moving beyond productivism, toward what Saito calls "degrowth communism." This reading relies heavily on unpublished notebooks and is [Contested]. Critics argue Saito over-interprets fragmentary notes and downplays the productivist thrust of Marx's published works.

## 7. Empirical Verification and Falsification

### 7.1 The Tendency of the Rate of Profit to Fall (TRPF)

**Status**: [Active debate] — One of the most empirically contested of Marx's predictions.

**The theory**: As capitalism develops, investment in constant capital (machinery, raw materials) grows faster than investment in variable capital (labor). Since only labor creates new value, the rate of profit (s/(c+v)) tends to fall over the long term, despite counteracting tendencies (increased exploitation, cheapening of constant capital, expansion of the reserve army of labor, foreign trade).

**Okishio Theorem (1961)**: The major formal counter-argument. If capitalists only adopt innovations that reduce costs, the general rate of profit **cannot fall** in equilibrium. This has been the most cited formal challenge to Marx's law. [Well-established]

**Empirical evidence — recent (2024–2025) studies**:

| Study | Country/Period | Finding |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| **Lopez-Espejo** (2025) | Spain 1960–2024 | Confirms TRPF: sustained rise in organic composition, slight decline in rate of surplus value, tendential decline in profit rate with cyclical fluctuations |
| **D'Acunto** (2024) | USA 1945–2016 | Confirms TRPF: ~3–4.5% long-term decline; interprets counter-tendencies as dynamic corrections in a complex adaptive system |
| **Fernandez Ruiz** (2024) | USA, UK, Japan | Confirms TRPF; also provides empirical **refutation of the Okishio Theorem**: technical change is associated with a _fall_ in profit rate when controlling for real wages — "The results of Okishio's theorem do not resemble reality" |
| **Zhou Zhaoyu** (2024) | USA post-WWII | Confirms TRPF; constructs a Marxist **long wave theory** based on the profit rate cycle explaining postwar booms and recessions |

**Theoretical refutations of Okishio**:

| Study | Argument |
|-------|----------|
| **Ankri** (2024) | Introduce socially solvent needs; a drop in average profit rate can occur even as all capitalists raise their individual profit rates, if organic composition increases sufficiently |
| **Ankri & Marcaggi** (2025) | Dynamic simulation; if even a small part of productivity gains translates to higher real wages, the average profit rate falls despite growing exploitation |
| **Le Van** (2025) | Leontief two-capital-good model; law does not require constant rate of exploitation; not a tautology |

**Meta-assessment**: [Active debate] The 2024–2025 evidence shows an **emerging consensus** among empirical researchers that the profit rate has trended downward in advanced capitalist economies. The theoretical dispute (Okishio vs. Marx) remains unresolved, though there is growing recognition that Okishio's theorem, while logically valid under its restrictive equilibrium assumptions, may not capture the dynamics of real capitalist economies.

**Source reliability**: High. Studies are from academic repositories (MPRA, arXiv), peer-reviewed journals (_World Marxist Review_), and university presses (Social Sciences Academic Press, Beijing). The Okishio theorem is from _Kobe University Economic Review_ (1961).

---

### 7.2 Labor Values and Market Prices — Empirical Correlation

**Anwar Shaikh** (_Capitalism: Competition, Conflict, Crises_, 2016): Across multiple countries and time periods, labor values explain typically **over 90%** of the variation in market prices. This is one of the most robust empirical findings in heterodox economics. [Well-established]

**Paul Cockshott**: Similarly demonstrated strong statistical regularities between labor content and price structures using input-output data from multiple economies.

**Farjoun & Machover** (_Laws of Chaos_, 1983): Argue that a deterministic, exact "transformation" is unnecessary; real capitalist economies are stochastic. We should look for **strong statistical correlations**, not deterministic derivations, and these correlations are empirically well-confirmed.

**Critics' rejoinder**: Correlation does not equal theoretical validity. The empirical correlation between labor values and prices may be a statistical artifact of input-output structures or reflect other factors (both are correlated with physical quantities). [Active debate]

## 8. Schools of Marxist Interpretation

### 8.1 Perry Anderson's Map of Western Marxism

**Work**: _Considerations on Western Marxism_ (1976)
**Status**: [Well-established] — the classic overview, though its central thesis is [Contested].

**Anderson's thesis**: Western Marxism is a structurally unified intellectual response to two related defeats:
1. The failure of proletarian revolutions in the West after WWI (Germany, Italy, Hungary).
2. The isolation of the Russian Revolution under Stalinism.

Blocked from active engagement in working-class struggles, Western Marxist theorists retreated from economics and political strategy into **philosophy, methodology, aesthetics, and culture**. They wrote in difficult prose for fellow academics, not workers — a sharp break from the praxis-oriented tradition of Marx, Engels, Lenin, Luxemburg, and Trotsky.

**Six major schools/traditions**:

| School | Key Figures | Focus |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| **Lukacsian** | Gyorgy Lukacs | Hegelian Marxism; reification, totality, class consciousness |
| **Gramscian** | Antonio Gramsci | Hegemony, civil society, organic intellectuals, war of position vs. war of maneuver |
| **Frankfurt School** | Adorno, Horkheimer, Marcuse, Benjamin, Fromm | Critical Theory; culture industry, negative dialectics, instrumental reason, aesthetics |
| **Sartrean / Existentialist** | Jean-Paul Sartre, Lucien Goldmann | Existentialism, phenomenology; mediation, praxis, progressive-regressive method |
| **Althusserian / Structuralist** | Louis Althusser, Etienne Balibar | Structuralism, Spinoza; epistemological break, overdetermination, ideological state apparatuses |
| **Della Volpean** | Galvano Della Volpe, Lucio Colletti | Scientific, empiricist Marxism; critique of Hegelian dialectics |

**Anderson's contrast — Classical vs. Western Marxism**:

| Classical Marxism | Western Marxism |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Unity of theory and practice | Theory divorced from practice |
| Political economy, state, revolutionary strategy | Philosophy, aesthetics, cultural critique |
| Written for workers and militants | Written for academic peers |
| Optimistic, forward-looking | Pervasively pessimistic |
| Economists, party leaders, organizers | Professional philosophers, literary theorists |

**Critique of Anderson** (Alex Callinicos, 1977): Anderson's framework is overly general; his turn toward orthodox Trotskyism as the alternative was itself an uncritical adoption that reproduced the very divorce between theory and class struggle he condemned.

**Source reliability**: Very high. Anderson's book (Verso/NLB) is a canonical reference, though it is recognized as a polemical synthesis rather than a neutral survey.

### 8.4 Value-Form Theory / The "New Reading of Marx"

**Key figures**: Hans-Georg Backhaus, Helmut Reichelt, Michael Heinrich, Chris Arthur, Fred Moseley; precursor: I.I. Rubin (1928)

**Core thesis**: The central theoretical contribution of Marx's _Capital_ is not the "labor theory of value" as a quantitative price theory, but the **analysis of the value-form** — the demonstration that in a commodity-producing society, social labor necessarily takes the form of value, and value necessarily takes the form of money.

**Key claims**:
- Marx's value theory is a **monetary theory of value**: value cannot exist independently of its expression in money.
- The transformation problem is a pseudo-problem generated by reading Marx through Ricardian (quantitative) rather than Marxian (form-analytical) lenses.
- The commodity fetishism section (Chapter 1, Section 4 of _Capital_ Vol. I) is not a supplement but the **theoretical core** of Marx's entire critique of political economy.
- Marx's project is a **critique** of classical political economy's categories, not a competing alternative within the same framework.

**Critique**: The value-form school has been criticized for abandoning empirical and quantitative concerns in favor of philosophical exegesis, rendering Marx's theory untestable and politically ineffectual. [Active debate]

### 8.5 Other Important Traditions

**Frankfurt School / Critical Theory**:
- **First generation** (Adorno, Horkheimer, Marcuse): Moved from political economy to the critique of instrumental reason, the culture industry, and the "totally administered society." The _Dialectic of Enlightenment_ (1944) is arguably the most pessimistic work in the Marxist tradition — arguing that Enlightenment rationality, including Marx's, contains the seeds of its own reversal into barbarism.
- **Second generation (Habermas)**: Shifted from the paradigm of production to the paradigm of communication; communicative action and the lifeworld/system distinction. Criticized Marx for conflating labor (instrumental action) with interaction (communicative action).
- **Third generation (Honneth)**: Turned to recognition theory; sees Marx's critique of capitalism as fundamentally about misrecognition and disrespect, not just exploitation.

**Open/Heterodox Marxism** (Werner Bonefeld, John Holloway, et al.): Rejects structuralist, deterministic, and economistic readings. Capitalism is a **class struggle** from below, not a structure that pre-exists it. The state is a form of capitalist social relations, not an instrument of a ruling class. Holloway's _Change the World Without Taking Power_ (2002) is the most influential statement of this tendency.

**World-Systems Theory** (Immanuel Wallerstein): Built on Marx and Braudel. Capitalism is a single world-system, not a stage in national development. Core/periphery relations are internal to capitalism, not a "pre-capitalist" externality. The modern world-system is in terminal structural crisis.

**Uno School** (Kozo Uno, Japan): A rigorous, non-teleological reading of _Capital_ as a pure theory of capitalism, sharply distinguished from historical and empirical analysis. Sekine (Albritton) developed this into a "dialectics of capital" approach.

**Dependency Theory** (Andre Gunder Frank, Theotonio Dos Santos, Ruy Mauro Marini): Applied Marxian categories to Latin American realities. Marini's theory of "super-exploitation" argued that dependency does not simply transfer surplus from periphery to center but transforms the value composition of labor in the periphery.

## 9. Marx and Contemporaries: Weber, Durkheim, Nietzsche

### 9.1 Marx vs. Max Weber (1864–1920)

**The relationship**: [Well-established] Weber explicitly positioned his work as an engagement with, and partial alternative to, Marx. He described his sociology of religion as an attempt to "test" the materialist conception of history.

| Dimension | Marx | Weber |
|-----------|------|-------|
| **Core problem** | Exploitation | Rationalization |
| **Method** | Dialectical materialism | Interpretive sociology (_Verstehen_) |
| **Capitalism's origin** | Primitive accumulation, expropriation | Protestant Ethic, this-worldly asceticism |
| **Stratification** | Class (economic, singular) | Class + Status + Party (three-dimensional) |
| **Modernity's future** | Revolution -> communism | "Iron cage" of bureaucracy, "disenchantment of the world" |
| **Bureaucracy** | Instrument of ruling class | Autonomous, self-perpetuating power structure |
| **Values** | Ruling ideas = ideas of ruling class | "Polytheistic" competition of values; science cannot adjudicate |

**Weber's critique of Marx**:
- Marx's economic determinism is **one-sided** — cultural and religious factors have independent causal efficacy (the _Protestant Ethic_ thesis is the paradigmatic counter-claim).
- Class is not the only axis of stratification; status (social honor) and party (political power) have independent dynamics.
- Socialism would not abolish domination but _intensify_ it — it would create a single bureaucratic hierarchy without the competitive checks of the market. This is Weber's famous "dictatorship of the official" thesis. [Contested]

**Marxist responses**:
- Weber's "class-status-party" trichotomy supplements rather than refutes class analysis — Marx never claimed class was the only form of social differentiation.
- Weber's prediction about socialism was formulated before any existing socialist state; the Soviet experience is a specific instance, not a universal confirmation.
- The Protestant Ethic thesis is complementary to, not contradictory to, a materialist account; Marx himself discussed the ideological preconditions for capitalist development.

**Source reliability**: Very high. Weber's works are canonical in sociology.

---

### 9.2 Marx vs. Emile Durkheim (1858–1917)

**The relationship**: Durkheim engaged Marx less directly than Weber, but his entire project is an implicit alternative.

| Dimension | Marx | Durkheim |
|-----------|------|----------|
| **Core problem** | Exploitation | Anomie / moral disintegration |
| **Method** | Historical materialism | Positivism — social facts as "things" |
| **Division of labor** | Source of alienation and class conflict | Source of **organic solidarity** and individual freedom |
| **Religion** | "Opium of the people" — ideological reflection of alienated social relations | The sacred/profane distinction is foundational to all social cohesion; even modern society needs collective beliefs and rituals |
| **Modern crisis** | Class exploitation producing revolutionary contradiction | Rapid change destroying moral regulation — anomie produces suicide, not revolution |
| **Remedy** | Abolition of private property; communist revolution | Professional ethics, corporative organization, moral education |

**Durkheim's implicit critique of Marx**:
- The division of labor is fundamentally **integrative**, not alienating — it creates interdependence and solidarity.
- Conflict is a **pathology** of insufficient moral regulation, not the motor of history.
- The socialist project of abolishing private property would not solve the moral problem of modern society — anomie is a problem of normative integration, not property relations.

**Marxist responses**:
- Durkheim conflates the **technical** division of labor (the differentiation of tasks) with the **social** division of labor (class divisions). Marx never criticized the former.
- Durkheim's notion of "organic solidarity" is ideological — what he calls interdependence, Marx calls the **mutual dependence of alienation**: workers are bound to capital not through solidarity but through the wage relation.

**Source reliability**: Very high. Durkheim's works are foundational in sociology.

---

### 9.3 Marx vs. Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900)

**The relationship**: Nietzsche was almost exactly contemporary with Marx (born 26 years later, died 17 years later). Nietzsche read Marx indirectly or not at all, but his thought is often read as an alternative radical critique of modernity.

| Dimension | Marx | Nietzsche |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| **Core problem** | Exploitation | Nihilism and internalized repression (_ressentiment_) |
| **Method** | Critique of political economy | Genealogy of morals |
| **Morality** | Ideological superstructure serving ruling class | Slave morality — the revenge of the powerless against the powerful; "good and evil" is a historical construct |
| **Religion** | Expression of earthly suffering; will be overcome with communism | "God is dead" — Christianity as the ultimate triumph of slave morality |
| **Freedom** | Liberation from alienated social relations through revolution | Self-overcoming (_Ubermensch_); creation of one's own values beyond good and evil |
| **The modern subject** | Aliented worker created by capitalism | The "last man" — the domesticated human who has internalized his subjugation |
| **The state** | Executive committee of the bourgeoisie | "The coldest of all cold monsters" |

**Points of convergence** (despite divergences):
- Both see modernity as a **crisis of dehumanization**.
- Both are **radical critics** of bourgeois morality and religion.
- Both practice a form of "unmasking" critique — revealing the hidden, unacknowledged forces (class interest / will to power) behind claims to universal truth and goodness.

**Points of divergence**:
- **Where is the problem located?** For Marx: in material/social structures (relations of production). For Nietzsche: in consciousness, morality, _ressentiment_ — in how we have learned to subjugate ourselves.
- **What is the remedy?** For Marx: collective revolutionary transformation of social relations. For Nietzsche: individual self-overcoming; the _Ubermensch_ who creates values beyond good and evil.
- **The status of "the masses":** For Marx: the proletariat as collective revolutionary subject. For Nietzsche: the herd, the rabble, the "many, the all-too-many."

**Nietzsche as resource for radical theory**: Despite Nietzsche's explicit anti-socialism, his critique has been appropriated by post-structuralist and post-Marxist thinkers (Foucault, Deleuze, Derrida) who argue Marx's humanism, his faith in the proletariat as a universal subject, and his historical teleology all remain trapped within the Enlightenment narratives that Nietzsche deconstructed.

**Marxist responses to Nietzsche**:
- Lukacs: Nietzsche is the philosopher of **irrationalism** — his destruction of reason serves reactionary political ends (though Lukacs' own engagement with Nietzsche was more complex than this summary suggests).
- Georg Brandes (contemporary of both): Tried to bridge the two but found them ultimately incompatible.
- Critical Theory: Adorno and Horkheimer incorporated Nietzschean themes (the self-destruction of Enlightenment rationality) while retaining Marx's commitment to emancipation.

**Source reliability**: Very high. Nietzsche's works are canonical. The comparison literature is extensive.

## 10. _Capital_ in the 21st Century

### 10.1 Current Academic Status

**Economic orthodoxy**: Marx's economics remains outside the mainstream of academic economics. Labor theory of value is not taught in standard economics curricula. The transformation problem is generally considered fatal by mainstream economists. [Well-established]

**Heterodox economics**: Marxian economics remains an active research program in heterodox journals (_Review of Radical Political Economics_, _Cambridge Journal of Economics_, _Historical Materialism_, _Science & Society_, _World Review of Political Economy_), with ongoing contributions to value theory, crisis theory, and empirical work on profit rates and inequality.

**Sociology and political science**: Marxian class analysis, world-systems theory, and theories of the state remain influential — often more so than in economics departments.

**Philosophy**: Marx is a canonical figure; _Capital_ is studied as a major work of philosophy, not just economics. The value-form school, the "New Reading of Marx," and post-structuralist interpretations keep Marx alive in philosophy departments.

**Public intellectual life**: _Capital_ saw a significant surge in popular interest after the 2008 financial crisis, with _Capital_ volumes briefly outselling many contemporary economics books. Piketty's _Capital in the Twenty-First Century_ (2013) is explicitly titled as a reference to Marx, though its theoretical framework is neo-classical/empiricist rather than Marxian.

### 10.2 Major 21st-Century Controversies

**1. The "Return to Marx" vs. "Marx Beyond Marx" debates**:
- **Antonio Negri & Michael Hardt** (_Empire_, _Multitude_, _Commonwealth_ trilogy): Marx's categories (value, exploitation, class) must be radically updated for "immaterial labor" and the "real subsumption" of society under capital. The "multitude" replaces the proletariat as revolutionary subject.
- **Moishe Postone** (_Time, Labor, and Social Domination_, 1993 — influential in 21st c.): A fundamental reinterpretation: Marx's critique is not of capitalism from the standpoint of labor but a **critique of labor in capitalism**. The goal is not the liberation of labor but liberation _from_ labor as a mediating social form. Traditional Marxism's affirmation of the proletariat is, for Postone, a form of "anti-capitalism from the standpoint of capital."

**2. Piketty and Marx**:
- **Thomas Piketty** (_Capital in the Twenty-First Century_, 2013; _Capital and Ideology_, 2019): Offers an empirical confirmation of Marx's insight that capitalism tends toward concentrated wealth (r > g), but without Marx's theoretical apparatus (labor theory of value, falling rate of profit, class struggle). Piketty's framework has been criticized by Marxists for abandoning Marx's structural analysis in favor of a purely distributional approach. [Active debate]

**3. AI, Automation, and Marx's predictions**:
- Marx's discussion of the "general intellect" in the _Grundrisse_ ("Fragment on Machines") has become one of the most cited 19th-century texts in 21st-century discussions of AI and automation.
- Marx predicted that as machinery absorbs living labor, the basis of value production collapses — producing a crisis not of poverty but of **overabundance** that capitalism cannot handle because it measures value in labor time.
- Critics argue this was an immature text (unpublished in Marx's lifetime, from the 1857-58 notebooks) and cannot bear the interpretive weight post-autonomist Marxists place on it. [Active debate]

**4. The Digital Economy and Value**:
- Are digital goods (software, data, content) governed by Marx's labor theory of value? If the marginal cost of reproduction approaches zero, how does value operate?
- **Christian Fuchs** (_Digital Labour and Karl Marx_, 2014): Argues that digital platforms reveal Marx's categories are more relevant than ever — users' unpaid "digital labor" is a new form of exploitation, the "audience commodity."
- **Critics**: This stretches Marx's definition of exploitation beyond recognition; watching ads is not the same as selling labor-power.

### 10.3 Academic Consensus and Persistent Divisions

[Scholarly consensus]:

| Area | Status |
|------|--------|
| _Capital_ is a major work of political economy | Consensus across disciplines |
| Marx's value theory is technically flawed (transformation problem) | Near-consensus in mainstream economics; rejected by Marxists |
| Marx's empirical predictions (immiseration, revolution in advanced countries) were largely incorrect | Broad consensus across all camps |
| The labor theory of value has been superseded by marginal utility | Mainstream economics consensus; rejected by Marxists |
| Marx's analysis of crisis and instability remains relevant | Broad consensus, even among critics |
| Class analysis remains useful | Broad consensus in sociology and political science |
| Marx's critique of alienation/commodity fetishism is philosophically profound | Broad consensus in philosophy and critical theory |

[Active debate]:

| Area | Status |
|------|--------|
| Whether value theory can be reformed or must be abandoned | Unresolved |
| Whether the falling rate of profit is empirically confirmed | Increasing evidence supporting it, but still contested |
| Whether Marx's vision of communism is compatible with ecological limits | Active, growing debate |
| Whether Marx was Eurocentric and if so, whether this is fatal | Unresolved; postcolonial critique has been met with substantial rebuttal |
| Whether dialectical methodology is essential or obsolete | Unresolved; divides analytic from continental traditions |
| Whether Marx's humanism persists or was abandoned | Tending toward "continuity in discontinuity" as intermediate consensus |

## Appendix: Typology of Critiques

### Distinguishing Academic Criticism from Ideological Attack

The following framework distinguishes scholarly engagement from polemical rejection:

| Criterion | Academic Critique | Ideological Attack |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Engages with Marx's texts** | Directly, with citations and attention to context | Through secondary summaries, "straw man" versions |
| **Acknowledges internal diversity** | Recognizes differences among Marxist traditions | Treats Marxism as a monolithic "doctrine" |
| **Specifies what is being criticized** | Targets specific arguments or claims | Condemns "Marxism" as a whole |
| **Acknowledges valid insights** | Even while criticizing, notes what Marx got right | Rejects everything Marx wrote |
| **Engages with responses** | Addresses Marxist counter-arguments | Ignores or dismisses all defenses |
| **Update mechanism** | Revises criticism in light of new scholarship | Frozen in time; repeats early 20th-c. criticisms |
| **Motive** | Understanding and truth-seeking | Discrediting and delegitimizing |

**Examples of academic critiques that meet these standards**: Bohm-Bawerk (engaged carefully with Marx's text), Samuelson (specified technical criticism), Schumpeter (acknowledged Marx as a sociologist even while criticizing his economics), Popper (distinguished Marx from other historicists), Kolakowski (drew on extensive textual knowledge), Arendt (engaged seriously with Marx's anthropology), Sperber (archival, contextual history).

**Examples that exhibit features of ideological attack**: Cold War-era treatments that reduced Marx to a "prophet of totalitarianism" without engaging his economic arguments; treatments that dismiss Marx as "disproven" without addressing ongoing research programs; treatments that attribute to Marx positions he explicitly rejected (e.g., crude economic determinism, denial of human agency).

**Caveat**: Even the most scholarly critiques have interpretive commitments and political implications. The distinction is one of degree and intellectual integrity, not a binary.
